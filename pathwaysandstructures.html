Pathways and Structures:

Overview:

Parkinson's disease involves the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc) which leads to the dysfunction within the basal ganglia, particularly affecting the direct and indirect pathways. (Note that these neurones produce dopamine, which is a neurotransmitter crucial for regulating mood, movement etc) This results in the characteristic motor symptoms associated with the disease, including tremors, rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and postural instability.

MAIN PART OF BRAIN AFFECTED: Basal Ganglia

Basal Ganglia:

A group of nuclei which are located deep inside the brain, which are involved in the regulation of voluntary motor movements, procedural learning, emotion + cognition

Loss of dopamine-producing neurones within the SNc disrupts the balance of neurotransmitters within the basal ganglia circuitry, specifically affecting the direct and indirect pathways.

Basal ganglia → responsible for muscle tone and ease of movement (and learning movement patterns)

    When signal for voluntary movement wants to be initiated, the cerebral cortex sends signals to basal ganglia (INPUT)
      Basal ganglia aids in the subconscious contraction of skeletal muscle tone + coordination of learned movement patterns
    Basal ganglia sends signals to thalamus first then back to (motor) cortex (OUTPUT), initiating these signals via spinal cord (controlled movement)
    In parkinson’s there is a problem with the output → not a normal controlled movement pattern

Pathways in Parkinson’s

Summary:

The loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra pars compacta which leads to the deficiency of dopamine (neurotransmitter that controls movement and coordination)

Normally Functioning Brain:

a. Dopaminergic Neurons: In a healthy brain, dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra produce dopamine.

b. Dopamine Synthesis: Dopamine is synthesized from the amino acid tyrosine through a series of enzymatic reactions, including the conversion of tyrosine to L-DOPA by the enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase (TH), and then the conversion of L-DOPA to dopamine by aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC).

c. Dopamine Release: When an action potential reaches the axon terminal of a dopaminergic neuron, vesicles containing dopamine fuse with the cell membrane, releasing dopamine into the synaptic cleft.

d. Dopamine Receptors: Dopamine released into the synaptic cleft binds to specific dopamine receptors on the postsynaptic neuron. There are several types of dopamine

e. Signal Transduction: Binding of dopamine to its receptors triggers intracellular signaling cascades that modulate neuronal excitability and neurotransmitter release, ultimately affecting motor control, reward, and cognitive functions.

f. Reuptake and Recycling: After neurotransmission, dopamine molecules not bound to receptors are taken back up into the presynaptic neuron by dopamine transporters (DAT) and repackaged into vesicles for future release.

The direct pathway:

Facilitates movement by promoting the initiation and execution of motor commands.

Involves excitatory projections from the striatum (specifically the caudate nucleus and putamen) to the globus pallidus interna (GPi) and substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr).

Dopamine normally acts on D1 receptors in the striatum, facilitating the direct pathway by disinhibiting the GPi/SNr. In Parkinson's disease, the reduced dopamine levels lead to decreased activation of the direct pathway, resulting in impaired initiation of voluntary movements.

Indirect Pathway:

helps to suppress unwanted movements and refine motor activity involves inhibitory projections from the striatum to the globus pallidus externa (GPe), which in turn inhibits the subthalamic nucleus (STN). The STN normally excites the GPi/SNr.

With decreased dopamine levels in Parkinson's disease, there is decreased inhibition of the GPe, leading to increased inhibition of the STN. This results in reduced excitation of the GPi/SNr, further inhibiting the initiation of voluntary movements.

Parkinson's Disease is associated with protein accumulation + aggregation, mitochondrial abnormalities and neuronal loss in the substantia nigra

a. Neuronal Degeneration: In Parkinson's disease, there is a progressive degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, leading to a significant reduction in dopamine levels in the brain.

b. Dopamine Deficiency: The loss of dopaminergic neurons results in a decreased synthesis and release of dopamine, leading to an imbalance in neurotransmission within the basal ganglia, a group of brain structures involved in motor control.

c. Motor Symptoms: The reduction in dopamine levels results in motor symptoms characteristic of Parkinson's disease, including tremors, rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and postural instability.

d. alpha synuclein is an abundant lipid-binding protein that is normally localised to presynaptic terminals and associated with synaptic vesicles and is a major components of the Lewy body. It is a diagnostic hallmark of PD.

    → It is a soluble protein and aggregates are formed by insoluble fibrillary deposits. (Note that familial PD can be due to mutations in the alpha synuclein genes).

Gross Examinations

A striking loss of pigmentation in substantia nigra in midbrain which correlates with loss of dopaminergic neurons.

Lewy bodies are considered a neuropathological hallmark of PD, they are not specific and rather a marker for “ill health" of a neuron.
